Chaza wa ghuba kwa chumvi ya uvinza.

Chaza wa Ghuba kwa Chumvi ya Uvinza is curated as an ‘maktaba mpaka mtaa’ experience.
The exhibition pairs archival materials (photographs, maps, official records,
newspapers, and personal collections) with newly created ‘then-and-now’ photography,
contemporary video loops, and site-specific sound. Together, these layers show how places
change - and how names travel with (or against) those changes.

The curatorial approach treats naming as both symbolic and practical. A place name can honor
a person, remember an event, describe a landscape, or signal a community’s identity. It can
also direct movement, concentrate commerce, and shape the reputation of a neighborhood.
Each selected name becomes an entry point to wider questions of culture, power, belonging,
and economy.

Why is this experience/exhibition/project important now?

This exhibition is urgent because Tanzania’s stories are moving faster than our archives. Across
generations, much of our social history is remembered through lived experience rather than
accessible records. Photographs, recordings, and documents remain scattered, unindexed,
privately held, or deteriorating, creating a gap between elders who lived the experience and
younger audiences are trying to understand it.

The ‘Mzizi ni Msingi’ reflection from within Tanzania’s creative community captures this moment
clearly: we need to unarchive the past, record and archive the present, and tell our struggles
and celebrations from our own perspectives - especially as youth culture and digital
Consumption accelerates, and imported narratives dominate public memory.

At the same time, a city like Arusha is changing rapidly. Growth, mobility, and the informal
economy reshape how people use streets and neighborhoods. Place names - official and
informal - continue to adapt with these shifts, capturing new moral, occupational, and social
meanings.

Finally, place naming is also becoming infrastructure: Tanzania’s national addressing and
postcode systems depend on street names and building identification to support physical
delivery of mail and wider economic and social services. In this context, names are not just
heritage - they are everyday systems that shape opportunity, access, and visibility.
